Rising like a forgotten relic of an older age, The Sepulcrum Institute stands at the edge of Calveris’ sprawling districts, where the city’s ordered structures bleed into tangled, forgotten alleys. Its architecture is unsettling—a grim fusion of austere stone walls and dark iron latticework, betraying no warmth or welcome. The outer facade is hewn from deep grey basalt, its surfaces smoothed by time yet marred with ancient cracks, as though the building itself strains against something unseen.

Thin, elongated windows punctuate its walls, too high to peer into, their glass darkened with soot and age. Iron braziers, mounted at precise intervals, burn with pale blue flames that neither flicker nor wane—perpetual and cold. A singular, double-doored entrance, framed by intricate carvings of intertwined serpents, stands at the top of five shallow steps, its doors marked with symbols long since worn unreadable.

Despite its imposing silence, there is a hum in the air here—not sound, but something deeper. A pressure in the skull, a sense of being watched, though no sentries stand guard. Those who approach feel the weight of unseen eyes pressing against their skin, and those who linger too long find their thoughts drifting, disjointed, as if the very air conspires to unravel their intentions.

No sign identifies the building. No patients enter, and none are seen leaving.
